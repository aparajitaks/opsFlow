1. INDUSTRIAL TRANSMITTER STANDARDS: THE 4-20MA CURRENT LOOP
To communicate telemetry data (temperatures, pressures, flow rates, and rotational speeds) reliably across the plant floor over distances up to 300 meters, opsFlow utilizes standard 4-20mA current loops.
- Advantage: Unlike voltage signals (0-10V) which suffer voltage drop over long wire runs and are highly sensitive to electromagnetic interference (EMI), current loops maintain constant current flow regardless of wire resistance.
- Zero-Point Baseline (4mA): Represented by exactly 4.0 mA. If a wire breaks or a sensor loses power, the current drops to 0.0 mA, allowing the controller to instantly detect an "Open-Loop Cable Break Fault".
- Full-Scale limit (20mA): Represented by exactly 20.0 mA, matching the maximum engineering range of the calibrated sensor.

---

2. SENSOR SPECS AND SIGNAL OUTPUTS
The opsFlow equipment array contains three primary telemetry sensor classes:

Sensor 2.1: Resistance Temperature Detectors (Pt100 RTDs)
Pt100 sensors consist of a pure platinum coil that exhibits a nominal electrical resistance of exactly 100.0 Ohms at 0°C. Resistance increases linearly with temperature at a rate of +0.385 Ohms per °C.
- Operating Range: Calibrated from -50°C to +200°C.
- Diagnostic Values:
  * At 0°C, resistance must read exactly 100.0 Ohms.
  * At 100°C, resistance must read exactly 138.5 Ohms.
  * Open Circuit: Resistance reads infinite (> 1,000,000 Ohms), indicating a broken sensor wire.
  * Short Circuit: Resistance reads near 0 Ohms, indicating a crushed cable housing.

Sensor 2.2: Pressure Transducers (Model: Trans-Press-10)
A strain-gauge based pressure transducer that measures hydraulic pressure from 0 bar to 200 bar, outputting a linear 4-20mA signal.
- Scaling:
  * 0 bar = exactly 4.0 mA output.
  * 100 bar = exactly 12.0 mA output.
  * 200 bar = exactly 20.0 mA output.

Sensor 2.3: Thermocouples (Type K)
Consists of two dissimilar metal wires (Chromel and Alumel) joined at one end, generating a tiny, high-temperature millivolt (mV) signal based on the temperature difference between the hot junction and cold junction (Seebeck effect).
- Operating Range: Up to 1200°C. Highly durable but sensitive to signal noise.

3. STEP-BY-STEP CALIBRATION OF 4-20MA TRANSMITTERS
To calibrate an analog pressure or temperature transmitter, technicians must perform a two-point tuning (Zero and Span adjustment) using a calibrated loop simulator (Model: Loop-Sim-V3):

- [ ] Step 3.1: Connect Loop Simulator and DMM
1. Perform LOTO on the cabinet control breaker.
2. Disconnect the sensor signal wires from the analog input card on the PLC.
3. Connect the loop simulator in series with a digital multimeter (DMM) set to measure DC current (mA).

- [ ] Step 3.2: Perform Zero Calibration (4.0 mA Setting)
1. Apply the baseline physical input (e.g., 0.0 bar pressure using a hydraulic hand pump, or place the Pt100 probe in an ice bath at exactly 0.0°C).
2. Read the current output on the multimeter.
3. If the meter does not read exactly 4.00 mA, locate the small, brass potentiometer screw labeled "ZERO" on the transmitter housing.
4. Rotate the ZERO screw slowly using a non-conductive adjustment tool until the multimeter reads exactly 4.00 mA.

- [ ] Step 3.3: Perform Span Calibration (20.0 mA Setting)
1. Apply the full-scale physical input (e.g., pump the hand pressure calibrator to exactly 200.0 bar, or place the Pt100 probe in a dry-block calibrator heated to exactly 200.0°C).
2. Read the current output on the multimeter.
3. If the meter does not read exactly 20.00 mA, locate the brass potentiometer screw labeled "SPAN" on the transmitter body.
4. Rotate the SPAN screw slowly until the multimeter reads exactly 20.00 mA.
5. Re-check the Zero point (Step 3.2), as adjusting span can shift the zero baseline slightly. Repeat until both points read exactly 4.00 mA and 20.00 mA.

4. DIAGNOSTIC PROCEDURES FOR SIGNAL NOISE & GROUND LOOPS
If a sensor output exhibits rapid fluctuations, sudden random spikes, or constant offset errors on the HMI (e.g., temperature reading jumps between 45°C and 72°C in milliseconds), execute these signal noise diagnostics:

Diagnostic Step 4.1: Identify Ground Loop Contamination
A ground loop occurs when a sensor cable shield or signal wire is grounded at multiple, physically separate points. Because different grounding plates in a factory can have a potential difference of several volts, current flows through the shield, inducing high-frequency AC electrical noise into the signal wires.
- Verification:
  1. Disconnect the cable shield wire from the local transmitter housing terminal.
  2. Measure the AC voltage between the disconnected shield wire and the transmitter metal casing.
  3. If the meter reads any AC voltage (> 0.5V AC), a ground loop exists.
  4. Correction: Ensure all shielded signal cables are grounded at ONE end only—preferably at the central PLC control cabinet grounding bus bar. Isolate the sensor end shield braid using electrical heat-shrink tubing to prevent physical contact with the machine chassis.

Diagnostic Step 4.2: Enforce Proper Shielding and Conduit Segregation
- Cable Separation: Sensor signal wires carrying low-voltage 24V DC or 4-20mA signals must never be routed inside the same physical metal conduit or wire tray as high-voltage AC motor power lines (480V lines from VFDs). High-voltage lines generate intense electromagnetic fields that induce electrical noise.
- Conduit Enclosure: If low-voltage signal cables and high-voltage power lines must cross, they must cross strictly at a 90-degree right angle to minimize electromagnetic coupling, and maintain a minimum physical clearance distance of exactly 300 mm.
